# 120. Origin

**Status:** Draft

---

# Question

Where did the Capability Evidence Model (CEM) come from?

---

# Purpose

This chapter documents the origin of CEM.

Its purpose is not to justify the framework, but to explain the observation that made it necessary.

---

# Observation

While developing a portfolio over approximately fifty days, a recurring problem became apparent.

The work represented weeks of reasoning, iteration, experimentation, and documented development.

Yet every hiring mechanism available expected that work to be summarized into a CV, a portfolio, or a short interview.

The more substantial the work became, the harder it became to communicate within the limited time available to a recruiter.

The problem was not a lack of evidence.

It was a lack of time to investigate that evidence.

---

# Problem

Existing hiring processes optimize for speed.

Candidates optimize for compression.

As a result, capability is often represented through summaries rather than through the evidence that produced it.

This creates a gap between what a person is capable of and what another person can reasonably evaluate.

---

# Chain of Reasoning

If capability cannot be communicated through increasingly shorter summaries, perhaps the problem is not communication itself.

Perhaps the problem is the expectation that capability should be summarized at all.

If evidence could be explored rather than compressed, this would fix two sides of the same failure, not one.

A candidate would get what a real conversation offers — patience, time, and back-and-forth — instead of being cut down to fit someone else's schedule.

An evaluator would receive something a summary can never give: evidence of actual skill, not a description of it.

Both failures come from the same root constraint: no time to actually investigate.

Patience and interactivity solve the candidate's side. Evidence-based assessment solves the evaluator's side.

They are not two separate fixes bundled together — they are the same fix, experienced differently depending on which side of the exchange a person is on.

That question was never really about hiring.

It was about the gap between capability and what can be communicated under a time constraint.

Hiring was simply the first place that gap became impossible to ignore.

The search for an answer to that question became the Capability Evidence Model.

---

# Conclusion

The search for an answer to that question became the Capability Evidence Model.
