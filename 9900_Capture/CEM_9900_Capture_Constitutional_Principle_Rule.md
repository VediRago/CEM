# Capture — Constitutional Principle Rule

**Date:** July 3, 2026
**Source:** Discussion following the addition of the Central Principle to `110_What_is_CEM.md`
**Belongs to:** `000_Method/00_CEM_Development_Method.md` — proposed as a new numbered rule.

---

## The rule

> **Constitutional Principle Rule.** CEM maintains a single constitutional principle from which the framework's authority is derived. Later chapters may elaborate, constrain, or apply that principle, but they should not introduce competing principles with equivalent authority. If a second principle appears necessary, the existing constitutional principle should first be re-examined to determine whether it is incomplete, rather than supplemented by a parallel authority structure. Only clear evidence that one principle is insufficient justifies creating more than one constitutional statement.

## Why this is the correct framing

The earlier framing of this rule ("there should only be one Central Principle") named a symptom, not the underlying discipline. A rule that only prohibits a specific label could be circumvented by introducing the same failure under a different name — a "Foundational Statement" or "Core Directive" sitting beside the Central Principle would recreate the exact problem while technically avoiding the word "principle." The real rule concerns *authority*, not vocabulary: CEM should have exactly one source from which later chapters derive their legitimacy, regardless of what that source is called.

This also gives the rule a correct failure mode. If a genuine gap is ever found — a case the existing principle doesn't actually cover — the correct response is not to add a second, parallel principle. It is to ask whether the original principle was incomplete and needs revision. That keeps authority singular even as the framework's understanding of its own mission grows, which is consistent with how every other structural change in this repository has been handled: found through use, resolved through re-examination, not patched around.

## Why this matters

The value of a single constitutional principle isn't the phrase itself — it's that every later chapter can derive its authority from one place without ambiguity about which source actually governs. A second, parallel authority — however well-intentioned or narrowly scoped — would quietly re-fragment the framework's authority at exactly the moment a single principle was established to prevent that.
