## CEM is not an AI framework (item 1)

CEM is an evidence investigation framework. AI is the current implementation that makes large-scale investigation practical — not the definition of CEM itself. This may need to be stated explicitly somewhere near [`110_What_is_CEM.md`](../100_Foundations/110_What_is_CEM.md) or in a dedicated Model chapter, since it prevents CEM from being permanently tied to one specific technology.

## Investigation replaces compression (item 2)

Restates and extends [`130_The_Problem.md`](../100_Foundations/130_The_Problem.md)'s existing argument — evidence is investigated before a human decision, rather than compressed in advance by the candidate. Likely belongs as connective tissue between [`130_The_Problem.md`](../100_Foundations/130_The_Problem.md) and the first Model chapter, not a standalone chapter of its own.

## The AI investigator's role, explicitly listed (item 3)

Its role: investigate evidence, ask follow-up questions, organize findings, verify where possible, identify recurring patterns, present transparent reports. **It does not make the final decision.** This is a strong, concrete candidate for the opening of the first real Model chapter — it's close to a job description for the AI layer.

## Investigation is conversational; the conversation itself becomes evidence (item 5)

Questions emerge from the candidate's own work, not a generic script. Connects directly to the [Standardization vs. Depth](Standardization_vs_Depth.md) capture — this is the mechanism that makes open-ended discovery possible without becoming arbitrary.

## Claims are investigated, not accepted or rejected outright (item 6)

Claims get explored through examples, application, trade-offs, consistency, and supporting evidence — not a binary true/false judgment. Worth connecting to Evidence Requirements / Evaluation Lens chapters when written.

## Patterns of reasoning across multiple independent sources (item 7)

CEM doesn't claim to know how someone thinks from one data point — it identifies recurring patterns across multiple, independent pieces of evidence. Important companion to Observation vs. Conclusion: a single observation is not a pattern; a pattern requires repetition across independent sources.

## Cross-project analysis (item 8)

Evidence from repositories, portfolios, documents, and conversations can reveal recurring principles a recruiter would rarely have time to discover manually. This is close to what happened across tonight's conversation itself (Noeme, Founding Era, Joe, the portfolio, and CEM all showing the same underlying method) — a real, lived example worth using if this becomes a chapter.

## Consistency itself becomes evidence (item 9)

Frameworks, documentation, layering, revisions, and repeated methodologies are themselves evidence of capability — not just the content they contain. This is a genuinely interesting, slightly recursive claim: CEM's own repository is evidence of the kind of thinking CEM is trying to detect in others.

## Framework-first methodology (item 10)

A recurring pattern noticed across tonight's work: build the framework first, then build the content. This is process observation about how the repository itself has been built, not a claim about CEM's subject matter — may belong in the Development Diary rather than the Model.

## Framework evolution through use (item 11)

Restates Rule 8 in different words — frameworks should improve through real use, and weaknesses discovered while applying them become evidence for refinement. Already well-established in [`00_CEM_Development_Method.md`](../000_Method/00_CEM_Development_Method.md) and proven twice tonight (the Blueprint's own v1.1 → v1.2 evolution). Likely doesn't need a new chapter — just a cross-reference.

## Company-specific models, one shared framework (item 12)

There is one CEM framework; each organization builds its own evaluation model on top of it. Consistent with, and probably belongs inside, whichever chapter resolves the [Five-Layer Architecture](CEM_9900_Capture_Five_Layer_Architecture.md) conflict — this is the "Company values" layer described from a different angle.

## Both sides benefit (item 18)

Candidates gain time to explain; evaluators gain investigated evidence. Restates Origin's two-sided-fix reasoning. No new chapter needed — could be a pull-quote used elsewhere (README, or a future public-facing summary).

## Additional/incidental observations (item 19)

Even if a candidate is not a match for the specific role being evaluated, CEM may surface recurring strengths that could be valuable elsewhere in the organization. Interesting operational idea — worth holding for a future Model or Actors chapter (relevant to `500_Actors/`, since this concerns what value the Company actor gets beyond a single yes/no decision).

## The investigation dossier (item 20)

Proposed output: Evidence, Observations, Confidence, Unresolved questions, Patterns, Verification, Additional observations. This is a concrete, structural proposal for what CEM's output actually looks like — a strong candidate for its own dedicated Model chapter once Observation vs. Conclusion and the layer architecture are both resolved, since the dossier format needs to obey both.

## Confidence grows with repetition (item 21)

Recurring evidence across multiple independent projects increases confidence in an identified capability. Connects to item 7 (patterns across independent sources) — likely the same underlying mechanism, described from two angles across the conversation.

## Patience at scale (item 22)

CEM attempts to give every candidate the patience and investigation normally reserved for a small number of finalists. This is a strong, human, motivating restatement of CEM's core value proposition — good candidate for pull-quote use, not necessarily its own chapter.

## Recurring personal design philosophy (item 23)

Across Narrative Design, UX, the Chapter Blueprint, and CEM itself, one principle repeatedly appeared: "Preserve complexity. Organize it so people can understand it. Not reduce complexity. Organize it." This was noticed as a pattern across tonight's actual work, not asserted in the abstract.

## Capability discovered about the author, through CEM's own process (item 24)

"The candidate repeatedly develops frameworks that preserve complex information while making it easier for others to understand, investigate, navigate, and build upon." Stated as a conclusion that emerged from accumulated evidence rather than self-description.

**Flag, not yet resolved:** Item 24 is, by CEM's own Observation vs. Conclusion rule (see the [dedicated capture note](CEM_9900_Capture_Observation_vs_Conclusion.md)), a *conclusion*, not an observation — and it was generated inside CEM's own development process, about CEM's own author, without the human-interpretation step the framework itself insists on elsewhere. Worth deciding deliberately whether this is legitimate self-reflection (a person and an AI reasoning together, informally, outside the formal CEM mechanism) or an example of the exact violation Section 14/17 warns against, surfaced accidentally inside the repository that defines the rule. This is worth its own honest discussion, not a quiet pass-through.

## Central Principle (as stated in the source discussion)

> CEM does not try to determine who is best. It reduces uncertainty by investigating capability through evidence and presenting transparent findings to the person making the decision.

This is a strong, quotable restatement of CEM's core purpose and may be worth formalizing as the framework's actual guiding principle statement, alongside or replacing "A résumé is a claim. CEM evaluates evidence." Worth comparing the two directly when there's time to decide which does more work.
